Section 1

Partitioning the Range

While Riemann integration slices the domain ($x$-axis) into small intervals, Lebesgue integration slices the **range** ($y$-axis).

Instead of the area of tall, skinny rectangles, we sum the measure of the set of $x$ values that output a certain $y$.

The Analogy (The Bill Counter)

ðŸ’µ Simple: The Bill Counter. Imagine a cashier counting a drawer full of mixed bills.
**Riemann:** Pick up each bill in order and add its value.
**Lebesgue:** Group all the $1s, all the $5s, all the $10s. The total is $(1 \times \text{count}_1) + (5 \times \text{count}_5) + \dots$.
The "count" is the **measure** of the set.

Section 2

Simple Functions

A **Simple Function** is a finite linear combination of indicator functions of measurable sets:

$$ \phi(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \mathbf{1}_{E_i}(x) $$

The integral of a simple function is easy: $\int \phi \, d\mu = \sum a_i \mu(E_i)$. The Lebesgue integral of any positive function is defined as the supremum of the integrals of all simple functions less than it.

Section 3

Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT)

If $f_n$ is a sequence of non-negative measurable functions such that $f_n \uparrow f$ pointwise, then:

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu $$

In other words, you can swap the limit and the integral if the functions are increasing and positive.

Section 4

Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT)

If $f_n \to f$ pointwise and all $f_n$ are "kept in check" by some integrable function $g$ (i.e., $|f_n| \leq g$), then:

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu $$

Why this is the GOAT

This theorem is used everywhere in Probability and ML (like swapping derivatives and expectation in Gradient Descent proofs). It guarantees that if the functions don't "explode" to infinity, the limit of the area is the area of the limit.

Section 5

Fatou's Lemma

For any non-negative measurable functions $f_n$:

$$ \int \liminf f_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf \int f_n \, d\mu $$

It's the "worst case" guarantee. The integral of the smallest possible limit is less than or equal to the smallest possible limit of the integrals. Area can vanish in the limit, but it can't spontaneously appear.

Section 6

Python: Numerical Lebesgue (Y-Slicing)

Approximating an integral by slicing the Y-axis into buckets.

Python Lebesgue-Style Summation 
import numpy as np

def f(x):
    # Let's integrate x^2 from 0 to 1
    return x**2

# Riemann approach (X-slicing)
x_slices = np.linspace(0, 1, 1000)
riemann_sum = np.sum(f(x_slices)) * (1/1000)

# Lebesgue approach (Y-slicing)
# We partition the RANGE [0, 1]
y_slices = np.linspace(0, 1, 100)
lebesgue_sum = 0
for i in range(len(y_slices) - 1):
    y_lower = y_slices[i]
    y_upper = y_slices[i+1]
    
    # We find the 'measure' of x such that f(x) is in this bucket
    # For f(x)=x^2, x = sqrt(y)
    measure = np.sqrt(y_upper) - np.sqrt(y_lower)
    lebesgue_sum += y_lower * measure

print(f"Riemann: {riemann_sum:.6f}")
print(f"Lebesgue: {lebesgue_sum:.6f}")
print(f"Theoretical: {1/3:.6f}")
