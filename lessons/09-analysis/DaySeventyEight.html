Section 1

The Foundation of Rigor

We began by fixing the holes in our intuition. The **Completeness Axiom** is what separates a world of approximations (rationals) from a world of continuous truth (reals). We formalized limits using **$\epsilon$-$\delta$**, turning "getting close" into a game of mathematical precision.

Key Takeaways

  • **Supremum/Infimum:** The better version of Max/Min.
  • **Cauchy Sequences:** Why $\mathbb{R}$ is the space where limits actually live.
  • **Topology:** Using open balls to define "nearness" in any space.
Section 2

Measure Theory & Lebesgue

We saw that Riemann's "boxes" fail on pathological sets like the rationals. **Lebesgue Measure** gave us a way to speak about the "size" of any outcome space, enabling modern probability.

The **Dominated Convergence Theorem** is the crown jewel here, allowing us to safely swap limits and integrals—a fundamental operation in almost every theoretical CS paper.

Section 3

Functional Analysis

We treated functions as vectors in **Hilbert Spaces**. This abstraction is the backbone of:

  • **Fourier Analysis:** Decomposing time into frequency.
  • **Kernel Methods:** Mapping data into infinite dimensions to find patterns.
  • **Quantum Computing:** Where states are vectors and operations are operators.
Section 4

Information & Entropy

Finally, we quantified "surprise." **Shannon Entropy** and **KL Divergence** aren't just statistics; they are the fundamental limits of how much we can know, compress, and learn.

Section 5

Analysis Capstone: Signal Information

Implement a Python pipeline that:

  1. Generates a complex signal (sum of frequencies).
  2. Calculates the **Entropy** of the time-domain signal.
  3. Calculates the **Fourier Transform** to move to the frequency domain.
  4. Calculates the **Entropy** of the frequency distribution.
  5. Compares the two—where is the information more "concentrated"?
