Section 0

Why Limits Matter

Calculus is the study of continuous change. But algebra only handles static values. The Limit is the tool that bridges this gap, allowing us to ask "what happens as we get arbitrarily close to a moment in time?"

üçï Simple: Imagine trying to slice a pizza into more and more slices.
8 slices... 16 slices... 1,000,000 slices.
The limit as the number of slices approaches infinity is that each slice becomes a single line, and the crust becomes a perfect circle.
Section 1

Intuition

\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L

Translation: "As $x$ gets closer and closer (but not equal) to $c$, the value of $f(x)$ gets closer and closer to $L$."

Left vs Right:
$\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x)$ (Left-hand limit: approaching from below)
$\lim_{x \to c^+} f(x)$ (Right-hand limit: approaching from above)
The limit exists IF AND ONLY IF Left Limit = Right Limit.
Section 2

The Formal Definition (Epsilon-Delta)

This is the rigorous standard logic.

Epsilon-Delta Definition
\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0 \text{ s.t. } 0 < |x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \epsilon
üéØ Simple: The Target Game
  • Challenger ($\epsilon$): Picks a target size epsilon around $L$. "You must be within 0.01 of the answer."
  • Defender ($\delta$): Must find a range delta around $c$. "Okay, if $x$ is within 0.005 of $c$, then $f(x)$ is inside your target."
  • If the Defender can always win, no matter how small the Challenger makes the target, the limit exists.
Section 3

Computing Limits

1. Direct Substitution

If the function is nice (continuous), just plug in the number.

\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1) = 3(2) + 1 = 7

2. Factoring (0/0 Case)

If you get $0/0$, there is a hole in the graph. Factor it out!

\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} \newline = \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} \newline = \lim_{x \to 2} (x+2) = 4

3. Limits at Infinity

Look at the highest power (similar to Big-O!).

\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x^2 + 5}{3x^2 - x} \newline = \frac{2}{3} \quad (\text{Since } x^2 \text{ dominates})
Section 4

Continuity & IVT

Continuity Checklist: A function $f$ is continuous at $c$ if:
  1. $f(c)$ is defined.
  2. $\lim_{x \to c} f(x)$ exists.
  3. $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$.
In plain English: "You can draw it without lifting your pen."

Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)

If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, and $f(a) < 0$ and $f(b)> 0$, then there MUST be a point $c$ where $f(c) = 0$.
