Section 1

Law of Large Numbers (LLN)

As the sample size $n$ increases, the sample mean $\bar{X}$ converges to the true population mean $\mu$.

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| < \epsilon)=1 $$

Example: Flip a coin 10 times, you might get 70% Heads. Flip it 10,000 times, you'll get very close to 50%.

Section 2

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The Magic Formula

The sum (or average) of many independent random variables—regardless of their original distribution—will tend toward a Normal Distribution.

$$ \bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}) $$

This is why the Bell Curve is everywhere.

Section 3

Why it Matters

  • Statistics is Possible: We can estimate population limits with small samples because we know the error is Normally distributed.
  • Noise Cancellation: Summing noisy signals averages out the noise (LLN).
  • Quality Control: Factory errors tend to be Normal.
Section 4

Python: Visualizing CLT

Summing Uniform Distributions to create a Bell Curve.

Python CLT Simulation 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n_samples = 10000
n_dice = 50 # Summing 50 dice rolls

# 1. Generate Data (Uniformly distributed ints)
# Shape: (10000, 50)
rolls = np.random.randint(1, 7, (n_samples, n_dice))

# 2. Sum each row
sums = np.sum(rolls, axis=1)

# 3. Plot Histogram
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.hist(sums, bins=50, density=True, color='purple', alpha=0.7, edgecolor='black')
plt.title(f'Distribution of Sum of {n_dice} Dice Rolls')
plt.xlabel('Sum')
plt.ylabel('Frequency')
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()

# Conclusion: The result looks like a perfect Bell Curve, 
# even though individual dice are Uniform probability.
