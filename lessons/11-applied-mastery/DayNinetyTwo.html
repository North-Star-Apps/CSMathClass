Section 1

Betting on the Future

An **Option** is a contract that gives you the *right* (but not the obligation) to buy or sell an asset at a specific price (**Strike Price**) on or before a certain date.

Financial math's greatest challenge was: **What is this contract worth today?** To answer this, we need to model how stock prices move over time.

Section 2

Log-Normal Random Walks

We assume stock prices follow **Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM)**. This means the *logarithm* of the price follows a normal distribution with a certain "drift" ($\mu$) and "volatility" ($\sigma$).

$$ dS_t = \mu S_t dt + \sigma S_t dW_t $$

Where $W_t$ is a Wiener process (Standard Brownian Motion).

Section 3

The PDE of Wealth

Black, Scholes, and Merton showed that you can create a "riskless" portfolio by constantly rebalancing the stock and the option. This leads to a Partial Differential Equation:

$$ \frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2} + rS \frac{\partial V}{\partial S} - rV = 0 $$
Section 4

The Solution

Solving the PDE for a Call option gives the famous formula:

$$ C = S_0 N(d_1) - K e^{-rt} N(d_2) $$

Where $N(\cdot)$ is the CDF of the standard normal distribution and $r$ is the risk-free rate.

Section 5

Measuring Sensitivity

Traders monitor "The Greeks" to see how their profit changes with small market movements:

  • **Delta ($\Delta$):** Change in price w.r.t. stock price.
  • **Gamma ($\Gamma$):** Change in Delta (acceleration).
  • **Theta ($\Theta$):** Change in price w.r.t. time (time decay).
  • **Vega:** Change in price w.r.t. volatility ($\sigma$).
Section 6

Python: Black-Scholes Implementation

Let's calculate the price of a Call option using the standard formula.

Python Option pricer 
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm

def black_scholes_call(S, K, T, r, sigma):
    """
    S: Current stock price
    K: Strike price
    T: Time to maturity (years)
    r: Risk-free rate
    sigma: Volatility
    """
    d1 = (np.log(S / K) + (r + 0.5 * sigma**2) * T) / (sigma * np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma * np.sqrt(T)
    
    call_price = S * norm.cdf(d1) - K * np.exp(-r * T) * norm.cdf(d2)
    return call_price

# Scenario: $100 stock, $105 strike, 1 year left, 5% interest, 20% vol
price = black_scholes_call(100, 105, 1, 0.05, 0.2)
print(f"Call Option Price: ${price:.2f}")

# Sensitivity to volatility
high_vol = black_scholes_call(100, 105, 1, 0.05, 0.5)
print(f"High Volatility Price: ${high_vol:.2f}")
