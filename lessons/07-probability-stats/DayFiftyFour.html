Section 1

Joint PMF & PDF

When studying two RVs $X$ and $Y$ simultaneously, we use a Joint Distribution.

Discrete (PMF)

$$ p(x, y) = P(X=x, Y=y) $$

Continuous (PDF)

$$ P((X,Y) \in A) = \iint_{A} f(x, y) dx dy $$

Section 2

Marginal Distributions

The Marginal Distribution of $X$ is the distribution of $X$ ignoring $Y$. We find it by "summing out" (discrete) or "integrating out" (continuous) the variable we don't want.

$$ p_X(x) = \sum_{y} p(x, y) $$ $$ f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) dy $$
üë• Simple: The Shadow Analogy
If the joint distribution is a 3D blob, the marginals are the 2D shadows cast on the $X$ and $Y$ axes. You lose information about the interaction, but you see the individual shapes.
Section 3

Conditional & Independence

Just like events, random variables can be conditional or independent.

Conditional: $$ f(x|y) = \frac{f(x, y)}{f_Y(y)} $$
Independence: $$ X \perp Y \iff f(x, y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y) $$
Section 4

Covariance & Correlation

How do $X$ and $Y$ "vary together"?

  • Covariance: $Cov(X, Y) = E[(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])]$.
    • $>0$: $X$ and $Y$ increase together.
    • $<0$: One increases as the other decreases.
  • Correlation ($\rho$): Normalized covariance (range $-1$ to $1$).

    $$ \rho = \frac{Cov(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y} $$

‚ö†Ô∏è Correlation != Causation:
Also, $Cov(X, Y) = 0$ implies they are uncorrelated, but not necessarily independent (they could have a non-linear relationship).
