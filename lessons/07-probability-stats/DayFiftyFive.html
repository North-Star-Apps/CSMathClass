Section 1

Law of Large Numbers (LLN)

The LLN states that as a sample size grows, its mean gets closer to the average of the whole population.

$$ \bar{X}_n \to E[X] \text{ as } n \to \infty $$
  • Weak LLN: Convergence in probability.
  • Strong LLN: Convergence almost surely.
üé∞ Simple: The Casino Hook
In a single hand, you might beat the dealer. But over 10,000 hands, the house edge is mathematically guaranteed to prevail. The "average" outcome is inevitable.
Section 2

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The CLT is the reason the Normal distribution is everywhere. It states that the sum (or mean) of many independent random variables will be normally distributed, regardless of the original variables' distribution!

$$ \sqrt{n} \left( \bar{X}_n - \mu \right) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2) $$
üèîÔ∏è Simple: The Galton Board
Even if you start with weird, non-bell-shaped distributions, adding them up enough times results in that beautiful symmetrical curve.
Section 3

The Z-Transform

To use the CLT in practice, we shift and scale our sample mean to match the Standard Normal ($Z \sim N(0,1)$).

$$ Z = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} $$

This allows us to calculate $p$-values and confidence intervals (which we will cover in the next section).

Section 4

Types of Convergence

In calculus, limits are simple. In probability, we have different ways variables can "get close":

  • In Distribution ($d$): The shapes of the curves match.
  • In Probability ($P$): The chance they differ by more than $\epsilon$ goes to zero.
  • Almost Surely ($a.s.$): They eventually become the same thing for sure.
Section 4.5

Python: LLN & CLT Demo

Simulating convergence and the Central Limit Theorem.

Python Limit Theorems 
import numpy as np
import random

# 1. Law of Large Numbers (LLN)
# Rolling a die 10,000 times. Expectation = 3.5
rolls = np.random.randint(1, 7, 10000)
running_mean = np.cumsum(rolls) / np.arange(1, 10001)

print(f"First 5 Means: {running_mean[:5]}")
print(f"Final Mean: {running_mean[-1]:.4f}") # Converges to 3.5

# 2. Central Limit Theorem (CLT)
# Sum of 30 uniform variables (NOT normal individually)
# Repeated 1000 times
sample_sums = []
for _ in range(1000):
    # Sum of 30 random numbers between 0 and 1
    s = np.sum(np.random.uniform(0, 1, 30))
    sample_sums.append(s)

# resulting 'sample_sums' will be Normally Distributed!
print(f"Mean of sums: {np.mean(sample_sums):.2f}") # ~15.0
print(f"StdDev of sums: {np.std(sample_sums):.2f}")
