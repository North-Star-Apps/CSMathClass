Section 1

Sample Space & Events

To study randomness, we need a mathematical model. We start with the Sample Space ($S$ or $\Omega$).

  • Sample Space ($S$): The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
  • Event ($E$): Any subset of the sample space.
  • Outcome: A single element of the sample space.
üçï Simple: The Pizza Analogy
The Sample Space is a whole pizza.
An Event is a specific topping (or combination).
Individual Outcomes are the tiny atoms of flavor.
Probability is simply the percent of the pizza that a specific event covers.
Section 2

The Art of Counting

Probability often reduces to: How many ways can this happen? / Total possible ways?

Permutations ($P$)

Order matters.

$$P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}$$

Example: Placing 1st, 2nd, and 3rd in a race.

Combinations ($C$)

Order doesn't matter.

$$C(n, k) = \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$$

Example: Choosing a team of 3 from 10 players.

üîí Hardware: PINs vs. Lotto
Your iPhone PIN is a Permutation. 1-2-3-4 is different from 4-3-2-1.
A Lottery Draw is a Combination. If the numbers are 5-10-15, it doesn't matter which one came out of the machine first.
Section 3.5

Python: Counting Tools

Python's `math` and `itertools` libraries make counting easy.

Python Permutations & Combinations 
import math
from itertools import permutations, combinations

# 1. Math formulas (Numbers only)
n, k = 5, 3
perms = math.perm(n, k) # 60
combs = math.comb(n, k) # 10

print(f"P({n},{k}) = {perms}")
print(f"C({n},{k}) = {combs}")

# 2. Itertools (Generate actual items)
items = ['A', 'B', 'C']

# Permutations (Order matters)
# list(permutations(items, 2))
# [('A', 'B'), ('A', 'C'), ('B', 'A'), ('B', 'C'), ('C', 'A'), ('C', 'B')]

# Combinations (Order doesn't matter)
# list(combinations(items, 2))
# [('A', 'B'), ('A', 'C'), ('B', 'C')]
Section 3

Axioms of Probability

Probability isn't just "vibes"; it follows the Kolmogorov Axioms:

  1. Non-negativity: $P(E) \ge 0$ for all $E$.
  2. Unit Measure: $P(S) = 1$. (Something must happen).
  3. Additivity: For mutually exclusive events $A$ and $B$, $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$.
$$ P(A^c) = 1 - P(A) $$ $$ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) $$
Section 4

CS: Hashing & Randomness

Why do software engineers care about sample spaces?

1. Hash Collisions

When you store data in a Hash Map, you are mapping a large key space to a smaller array index space. Probability tells us how often two keys will hit the same index.

2. Randomized Algorithms

Algorithms like QuickSort or Skip Lists use a source of randomness to guarantee good average-case performance, even if the input is malicious.

üéÇ Simple: The Birthday Paradox
In a room of just 23 people, there is a >50% chance two people share a birthday.
This is why Hash Collisions are much more common than people expect!
