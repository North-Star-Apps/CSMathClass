Section 1

Field Extensions

A Field Extension $L/K$ (read "L over K") is simply a field $L$ that contains $K$ as a subfield.
Example: $\mathbb{C}$ is an extension of $\mathbb{R}$.

Degree of Extension $[L:K]$: The dimension of $L$ considered as a Vector Space over $K$.
Example: $[\mathbb{C}:\mathbb{R}] = 2$. Basis is $\{1, i\}$.

Analogy: Adding Dimensions
Think of $K$ as a line (1D). To get $L$, we "add" a new direction (a root like $\sqrt{2}$ or $i$).
$\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ consists of all number $a + b\sqrt{2}$. It's a 2D plane over the rational line.
We build fields by stacking dimensions.
Algebraic Element: $\alpha \in L$ is algebraic over $K$ if it is a root of some polynomial in $K[x]$.
Minimal Polynomial: The monic irreducible polynomial of smallest degree that has $\alpha$ as a root.
Section 2

Splitting Fields

Where do roots live?
The polynomial $x^2 - 2$ has no roots in $\mathbb{Q}$.
To find roots, we must extend the field to $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$.

Splitting Field: The smallest extension $L$ of $K$ containing all roots of a polynomial $f(x)$. In $L$, $f(x)$ "splits" into linear factors $(x - r_1)(x - r_2)...(x - r_n)$.
Example: $x^2 + 1$ splits in $\mathbb{Q}(i)$.
Example: $x^3 - 2$ splits in $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega)$, where $\omega$ is cube root of unity.
Section 3

The Galois Group

This is the core definition. The Galois Group $\text{Gal}(L/K)$ is the set of all automorphisms of $L$ that fix every element of $K$.

$$ \sigma \in \text{Gal}(L/K) \iff \sigma: L \to L \text{ is isomorphism AND } \forall k \in K, \sigma(k) = k $$
ðŸ”„
Analogy: Permuting Roots
Think of the roots of a polynomial as vertices of a shape.
The Galois Group describes the Symmetries of this shape.
For $x^2 + 1 = 0$, roots are $i, -i$. We can swap them ($i \to -i$) or leave them alone ($i \to i$).
This group is $C_2$ (cyclic group of order 2).

KEY INSIGHT: An automorphism must map a root to another root of the same minimal polynomial.

Section 4

Fundamental Theorem

Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory: There is a one-to-one correspondence (bijection) between:
  • Intermediate fields $M$ ($K \subseteq M \subseteq L$)
  • Subgroups $H$ of the Galois Group ($H \subseteq \text{Gal}(L/K)$)
The correspondence is inclusion-reversing: Smaller subgroups correspond to larger fields.
Fixed Field: Given subgroup $H$, the corresponding field is: $$ L^H = \{ x \in L : \sigma(x) = x, \forall \sigma \in H \} $$

This allows us to translate impossible field problems into solvable group theory problems.

Section 5

Unsolvability of the Quintic

Why is there no formula for degree 5?

  1. A polynomial is "solvable by radicals" iff its Galois Group is a "Solvable Group".
  2. The symmetric group $S_5$ (permutations of 5 roots) is NOT a solvable group. (Its simple components are non-abelian).
  3. Therefore, a general quintic equation (whose Galois group is $S_5$) cannot be solved by radicals.
Abel-Ruffini Theorem (1824): No general algebraic solution for polynomial equations of degree 5 or higher exists.
Section 6

Impossible Constructions

Ancient Greeks asked if we could do these with compass and straightedge:

1. Trisect an Angle: Impossible. Requires solving cubic $4x^3 - 3x - \cos(3\theta) = 0$. Roots of general cubic are degree 3. Compass operations only generate degree $2^n$ extensions. 3 is not a power of 2.
2. Doubling the Cube: Impossible. Requires constructing $\sqrt[3]{2}$. Degree 3.
3. Squaring the Circle: Impossible. Requires constructing $\sqrt{\pi}$. $\pi$ is transcendental (Lindemann, 1882), so degree is infinite.

Python Galois

Using sympy to find Galois Groups.

Python Galois Group Calculation 
from sympy import symbols, Poly
from sympy.polys.polytools import galois_group

x = symbols('x')

# Example 1: Quadratic x^2 - 2
# Roots: Â±sqrt(2). Group should be C2 (swap roots)
f1 = Poly(x**2 - 2, x)
G1 = galois_group(f1)
print(f"Group of x^2 - 2: {G1}")

# Example 2: Cubic x^3 - 2
